Mon cher Butler,
La lettre ci-jointe que je reçois du Professeur John L.
Gerig (Département des langues romanes à Columbia University),
est un témoignage précieux pour nous de l'action bienfaisante de
notre Conciliation avant et pendant la guerre.
Le hasard m’a fait rencontrer cet été, quelques instants,
le Pr. Gerig dans le train qui nous amenait à Reims. C'était
pour moi un voyageur inconnu. Il écoutait silencieux ce que me
disaient M.M. Croiset et d’autres membres de l'Institut qui
avaient assisté la veille à votre €très€ intéressante conférence
de la Cour de Cassation et ce que nous disions de l'éducation
qu’avait répandue, depuis des années, dans l'opinion américaine,
l'effort de la Dotation Carnegie et de la Conciliation.
A la fin, le voyageur inconnu sortit de son silence et dit
en substance ce qui suit:
C'est votre éducation pacifique, en Amérique, depuis vingt
ans, qui a préparé et déterminé l'entrée des Etats-Unis dans la
guerre contre le militarisme allemand.
Je fus ému de cette déclaration inattendue et je ne

cachai pas à mon interlocuteur qu’elle était pour moi une ré-
compense. Malheureusement je ne pus causer avec lui que quelques
instants et je dus le prier de m'écrire un jour, s’il le pouvait!
C'est ce qu’il vient de faire! J'en suis profondément touché et
je vous prierai, à l'occasion, de le lui dire.
Vous n’avez pas oublié que ces conférences auxquelles le
Professeur Gerig fait allusion, c’est l’invitation d'Andrew Car-
negie, en 1907, qui les a provoquées à Pittsburgh et à New York,
mais c’est vous avec Keppel qui les avez organisées sur toute
l'étendue des Etats-Unis, en 1911. Ce fut l'enseignement de notre
doctrine, répandue dans toute la jeunesse et dans tous les milieux
de l'activité américaine. Organisation et Défense de la Paix. Ces
deux mots n’en ont jamais fait qu’un pour nous. La guerre que
nous n'avons pas pu éviter à été l’application de cette doctrine.
Votre affectueusement dévoué.

Monsieur le Sénateur d'ESTOURNELLES de CONSTANT
PARIS.
My dear Sir,
Since our memorable visit to Reims I have thought it
just to let you know my impressions of what has been your role in
the important events of the past twenty years of which we witnessed
a fitting climax amidst the ruines or the above mentionned sacred,
city.
When I gratuated from the University of Missouri in 1898,
French influence was scarcely perceptible in that very teutonic
milieu. It is true that the Germany evoked was that of the idealis-
tic period of 1848, when Senator Carl Schurry, General Franz Siegel
and other exilés sought refuge in the United States. In 1899 I went
to the University of Nebraska to continue my studies for four years.
In that Scandinavian environment France was almost totally unknown.
My dear friend and teacher Hjalmar Edgren, who afterwards became
judge for American and English literature in the Nobel Institute,
was one of the rare exceptions. After spending two years of study
in France (1903-05), I returned to the United States to teach in
-2-
Williams College, Williamstown. Mass., When again German influence
predominated almost wholly. In 1907 I went to Columbia University
where I heard your lecture in Scherneerhorn Hall - the first time
I had an opportunity to hear a Frenchman make a public address in
the United States. I remember that you spoke in English because we
were unable to bring together sufficient members who could unders-
tand Franch. I remember also the very favorable impression you made
on your audience by the doctrines of universal peace which you sà
eloquently advocated. And I remember finally the numerous requests
I received for information concerning you, and how eagerly young men
were discussing the ideas you placed before them.
That date was, so to speak, the point de départ of your
influence in the intellectual cercles. Greater interest was taken,
as a consequence, in France ; and more conférenciers were invited
from your country. Briefly French culture became better known and
admired. The course of events was rapid. About 1910, the eminent
classical scholar Professor Paul Schorey, of the University of
Chicago, published a series of articles in the New-York Nation and
Evening Post in which he made manifest the superiority of French
scholarship over that of Germany, and advocated colser relations
between our Universities and those of vour beautiful country. Ex-
change professorship with France, which until then had not been
looked upon with great favour, assumed considerable importance.
The Hague Conference, of which you were generally considered - in
the United States at least - as the father and which was endowed
by a generous American, was vieved with enthousiasm as tending
-3-
to further the ideal of our to Republics. The German theory of
weltpolitik lost ground everywhere in our educational institutions,
and with it declined german influence.
So when the great war came our Universities had been
emancipated. But such was not the case with the public which conti-
nued to maintain friendly feelings for the twenty or more millions
of citizens of German origin as well as for Ireland's desire for
liberty so fervently and incessantly advocated by the equally huge
number of citizens of that nationality. To these could be added ano-
ther group consisting of as many millions who felt that the war was
brought about by imperialistic or selfish aims. Thanks to the very
skillfull and well organized German propaganda which the allied coun-
tries were wholly unable to meet.
But during all this time the intellectuals who had rallied
to your theories kept up a vigorous campaign. The importance of
their efforts cannot be overestimed, and so, in 1917, when our dis-
tinguished President J.W. Wilson, proclaimed the great war te be a
war to en wars, a war that was necessary to attain universal peace,
the Nation - in spite of ominous prophecies - heeded this clarion
call and arose at once to his support. In that splendid manifesta-
tion of a nation's idealism your labors of a life-time and those of
your co-workers, the American intellectuals, attained a glorious
fruition, with such enthousiasm did the United states enter the war
that when the collapse of the German autocraty took place some eight
millions of men were under arms. And to the present - notwithstan-
ding expressions of opinion to the contrary on the part of more or
less ill-informed individuals - this same spirit prevails, for the
-4-
average American the most blessed indemnity that can accrue from
the war is universal peace.
I hope you will kindly excuse this hasty hearty letter
which I desired to send you before my departure from France to-
morrow. If it serves for nothing else, at least it is the expression
of the admiration of one individual for a man who has always had
the courage to uphold his ideals.
yours very truly,
John L. GERIG
Assoc. Professor of Romance Languages and Celtic,
executive officer of the Department of Romance Languages.
